\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This section evaluates MPTA-Repair on benchmark and industrial
datasets. The evaluation follows the clock-bound repair setting
defined in Section~\ref{sec:approach}, where only numerical bounds in
guards and invariants are repairable. We compare MPTA-Repair with an
iterative baseline adapted from TARTAR
\cite{kolbl2019clock}.

\subsection{Evaluation Setup and Research Questions}

We evaluate MPTA-Repair on two datasets, summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:dataset-statistics}. The benchmark dataset includes nine
benchmark models selected from UPPAAL examples
\cite{behrmann2004tutorial} and the XtaBenchmarkSuite
\cite{farkas2018benchmarks}, such as Elevator and FDDI. The industrial
dataset constructed in this work contains five closely coupled NTA
case-study models: Gear Control System for automotive transmission
\cite{lindahl1998gear}, SAE-RoR for autonomous-vehicle road-junction rules
\cite{alves2021double}, Train Controller Alarm for railway emergency
timing \cite{gonzalez2024uppaal}, Train Gate Controller for
shared-resource concurrency \cite{alur1994theory}, and Pacemaker for
medical pacing \cite{jiang2010heart}. Compared with the benchmark
models, these case studies involve more tightly coupled timing
constraints across multiple properties.

Since real-world NTA models with labeled clock-bound faults are
difficult to obtain, we synthesize faulty instances using mutation
\cite{jia2011mutation}. Some collected models contain few or no
properties, so we supplement them with timed safety properties described
or implied by their design documents or related papers. For each model,
we seed modifications to all clock-constraint bounds by
$\{-10,-1,+1,+0.1M,+M\}$, where $M$ is the maximal bound compared
against a clock in that model. Because the combination space of
mutations is large, each mutant randomly modifies at most two
clock-bound occurrences. We retain only mutants that are syntactically
valid, violate at least one timed safety property, and preserve the
original functional behavior. Each repair instance is executed with a
10-minute timeout for both MPTA-Repair and the baseline.

The iterative baseline adapted from TARTAR \cite{kolbl2019clock}
repairs one violated property and its TDT at a time. If validation
exposes a new TDT, it starts another repair round rather than solving
all TDTs jointly.
The evaluation is organized around the following research questions:

\noindent\textbf{RQ1.} What are the main characteristics of the
benchmark and industrial repair instances?

\noindent\textbf{RQ2.} How does MPTA-Repair compare with the iterative
TARTAR-style baseline in terms of accepted-repair rate?

\noindent\textbf{RQ3.} How does iterative space optimization affect
repair efficiency and effectiveness?

\noindent\textbf{RQ4.} What practical lessons emerge from applying
MPTA-Repair to industrial cases, and what explains its advantages over
the iterative TARTAR-style baseline and its remaining failures?

\subsection{Results by Research Question}

\noindent\textbf{RQ1: Dataset and mutation characteristics.}
Table~\ref{tab:dataset-statistics} shows that the benchmark dataset
contains nine original models and 54 retained mutants, whereas the
industrial dataset contains five original models and 105 retained
mutants. Although the benchmark dataset includes more original models,
the industrial models are structurally larger and have more properties
per model. On average, the benchmark models contain 30.56 locations,
52.67 transitions, and 4.1 properties, compared with 57.80 locations,
91.80 transitions, and 7.3 properties for the industrial models. The
resulting repair instances therefore cover both smaller benchmark
models and larger industrial models with broader property sets.

\noindent\textbf{RQ2: Accepted-repair rate.}
Table~\ref{tab:overall-results} shows that MPTA-Repair achieves higher
accepted-repair rates than the iterative baseline on both datasets. On
the benchmark dataset, the baseline produces accepted repairs for 39 of
54 instances, corresponding to a success rate of 72\%, whereas
MPTA-Repair repairs 50 instances and reaches 93\%, an increase of 21
percentage points. On the industrial dataset, the baseline repairs 21
of 105 instances, while MPTA-Repair repairs 80, corresponding to
success rates of 20\% and 76\%, respectively. The improvement is
therefore substantially larger on the industrial dataset.

The average repair time should be interpreted together with the success
rate. On the benchmark dataset, MPTA-Repair is both more successful and
faster on average. On the industrial dataset, MPTA-Repair takes longer
than the baseline because it continues exploring joint repairs for
multiple properties and TDTs. The baseline often terminates earlier
after failing to find a valid candidate repair, which lowers its average
time but also leads to a much lower accepted-repair rate. This result
indicates that joint property-trace solving is especially useful for
industrial models, where timing constraints are more tightly coupled
and changing one clock bound may affect several properties at once.

\noindent\textbf{RQ3: Effect of iterative space optimization.}
Table~\ref{tab:search-optimization-results} compares MPTA-Repair with
and without search optimization. On the benchmark dataset, the average
time changes slightly from 15.74s to 15.46s because many faults are
already localized. On the industrial dataset, optimization reduces the
average time from 231.45s to 85.28s by reusing encoded obligations and
focusing solving on relevant property-trace constraints and repairable
bounds. The optimization does not change the repair space or the
accepted-repair condition, so it does not change which candidates are
accepted; its main effect is to reduce solving time.

\noindent\textbf{RQ4: Practical lessons and failure analysis.}
The industrial cases show that accepted repairs require both global
property checking and functional admissibility. A candidate that blocks
one TDT may still violate another property, and a bound edit may satisfy
timing properties only by removing intended functional behavior.
MPTA-Repair avoids these cases by requiring each candidate to satisfy
the full property set and pass the functional-equivalence check.

This also explains its advantage over the iterative TARTAR-style
baseline. The baseline repairs one property and one TDT at a time, so
it often produces local candidates that fail full-property regression or
admissibility checking on tightly coupled industrial models.
MPTA-Repair instead solves over a joint property-trace pool, which
better captures shared timing constraints. The remaining failures
mainly occur when no admissible candidate is found within the time
budget, especially in models such as Pacemaker where dense timing
interactions enlarge the clock-bound repair space.
