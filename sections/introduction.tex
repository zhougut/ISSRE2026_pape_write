\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Real-time and industrial software increasingly runs where timing faults can compromise safety,
availability, or mission success. Timed automata have modeled railway train-gate controllers,
communication protocols, and safety layers \cite{basile2021unisig}; automotive gear-control logic,
road-junction behavior, and time-bounded decisions \cite{lindahl1998gear}; medical pacemakers and
ventilator controllers with safety-critical timing windows and actuation deadlines \cite{jiang2012pacemaker};
and aerospace or avionics schedulability and deadline analysis \cite{david2010herschel}.
In these domains, correctness depends on event order and timing: an alarm,
pulse, message, or resource release may still be wrong if it occurs too
early, too late, or for an incorrect duration.

Timed automata model such systems \cite{alur1994theory}. Networks of timed automata use clocks,
guards, invariants, synchronizations, and resets to encode discrete behavior and quantitative timing
constraints \cite{bengtsson2004timed}. Verification checks safety, bounded response, deadline satisfaction,
error-state reachability, alarm duration, and deadlock freedom. When a property is violated,
UPPAAL, Kronos, or opaal can produce a timed diagnostic trace (TDT),
a timed counterexample showing a path to a violating state \cite{daws1996kronos}.

Diagnosis alone is insufficient. Industrial models contain many locations, transitions, clocks, guards,
invariants, synchronizations, and resets, so interpreting a TDT as a correct edit
remains difficult. A TDT exposes one feasible violating execution, but not the faulty
clock constraint, whether the defect is in a guard or invariant, or how
a bound should change \cite{koelbl2019clock}. Under multiple properties, ambiguity grows because
industrial specifications combine safety, response, scheduling, and reliability obligations \cite{vogel2022patterns}.
A local edit may remove one trace while leaving related traces feasible or
breaking another property, so each candidate must be validated against the complete property set.

Existing timed-automata repair techniques provide an important 
foundation. Clock-bound repair encodes a TDT as linear real arithmetic, 
introduces variation variables for clock bounds, and uses MaxSMT solving 
to compute small modifications to guards and invariants \cite
{koelbl2019clock}. TarTar builds on this idea by suggesting syntactic 
repairs and checking whether the repaired model preserves the untimed 
functional behavior of the original network timed automaton \cite
{koelbl2020tartar}. Related work includes parameter synthesis for parametric timed automata
\cite{alur1993parametric}, clock-guard repair through abstraction and testing \cite{andre2019repairing},
robustness analysis for uncertain timing constants \cite{bendik2021robustness}, and SAT/SMT-based model
or program repair \cite{jose2011bugassist}. These methods show feasibility, but most TDT-based workflows
still repair one violated property or diagnostic trace at a time.

This paper addresses multi-property interaction. A baseline repair loop can process one violated 
property, collect one TDT, compute a local repair, instantiate a 
candidate model, and run regression verification. However, this loop may become inefficient or 
unstable when a single timing fault affects  several properties or related traces. Similar 
traces may encode overlapping faulty behavior. A candidate that removes one trace may leave 
other violations feasible, and a bound change made for one property may affect another property 
when the same clock, transition, location, or synchronization appears in both executions. If 
all editable  bounds are treated as equally plausible repair targets, the solver may also 
explore weakly related candidates with limited relevance to the observed violations.

This paper presents MPTA-Repair, an automatic workflow for multi-property, multi-counterexample clock-bound repair
of industrial timed-automata models. Given a faulty model, timing properties, and diagnostic traces,
MPTA-Repair suggests small modifications to numerical clock bounds in transition guards and location
invariants. Its repair space is limited to clock-bound constants, excluding arbitrary structural edits,
specification changes, synchronization changes, and reset changes. Lightweight relation analysis among properties,
traces, and editable bounds guides search only as an optimization. Every accepted candidate must
satisfy the complete property set through regression verification and pass a TARTAR-style admissibility
check based on functional equivalence. Thus, MPTA-Repair targets system-level timing corrections, not
local counterexample patches.

We evaluate MPTA-Repair on two dataset groups: a benchmark dataset from UPPAAL and
XTA sources, and an industry dataset covering railway alarm and communication scenarios
\cite{gonzalez2024realtimedevs,basile2021unisig}, train-gate behavior \cite{behrmann2004tutorial},
automotive gear control \cite{lindahl1998gear}, cardiac pacing \cite{jiang2012pacemaker},
autonomous road-junction rules \cite{belguidoum2021junction}, mechanical ventilation \cite{cuartas2023ventilator},
and aerospace scheduling \cite{david2010herschel}. We measure repair effectiveness, repair size,
runtime, regression results, and behavior preservation, compare with an iterative TARTAR-style baseline
under identical full-property regression and admissibility requirements, and report practical observations.

The contributions are as follows. We formulate practical clock-bound repair as a multi-property,
multi-counterexample problem; present MPTA-Repair with diagnostic-trace and relation-guided search; implement a prototype
combining trace analysis, MaxSMT-based repair, full-property regression verification, and TARTAR-style admissibility checking;
evaluate it on benchmark and industry datasets against an iterative single-property baseline under identical
acceptance criteria; and report lessons on property selection, repair interpretability, and full regression
after each candidate repair.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section II provides
the background and problem setting, including timed automata, diagnostic
traces, clock-bound repair, and the multi-property repair objective.
Section III presents MPTA-Repair and its relation-guided repair
workflow. Section IV describes the prototype implementation and
evaluation setup. Section V reports the evaluation results and practical
lessons learned from the benchmark and industry datasets. Section VI
discusses threats to validity and related work. Section VII concludes the
paper.
