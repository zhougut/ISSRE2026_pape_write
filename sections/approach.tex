\section{Approach}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{Overview}

MPTA-Repair takes as input $M=(N,\Phi)$, where $N$ is an NTA and
$\Phi=\{\varphi_1,\ldots,\varphi_m\}$ is a set of timed safety
properties, and modifies only repairable numerical clock bounds in
transition guards and location invariants. It returns
$M'=(N',\Phi)$ that satisfies the accepted-repair condition in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:accepted-repair}; otherwise, it reports that no accepted
repair was found within the resource budget.

The properties in $\Phi$ may not be independent, as different
properties may be affected by the same clock bounds. Their TDTs may be
identical, share part of an execution path, or depend on the same
repairable bounds. A repair derived from one TDT may leave other
violations unresolved or cause a satisfied property to fail. Treating
TDTs separately ignores these relations and may lead to repeated,
conflicting repairs.

Figure~\ref{fig:mpta-repair-workflow-small} summarizes the MPTA-Repair
framework. Initial full-property verification either confirms that no
repair is needed or initializes a joint property-trace constraint pool
with TDTs.
MPTA-Repair analyzes relations among properties, TDTs, and repairable
bounds, removes redundant constraints, and solves constraints that share
bounds jointly. The constraints are encoded as a MaxSMT
problem, and the resulting assignment is applied to $N$ to construct a
candidate model. MPTA-Repair extends TARTAR's single-TDT MaxSMT repair
procedure~\cite{kolbl2019clock} by jointly processing related
constraints and using newly generated TDTs in subsequent repair
iterations.

Each candidate undergoes full-property regression verification and the
admissibility check based on functional equivalence. If regression
verification reveals new violations, the corresponding TDTs are added to
the joint pool. Any assignment that fails either validation step is
excluded from subsequent iterations. The process repeats until an
accepted repair is found or the time budget is exhausted.
Figure~\ref{fig:mpta-repair-workflow} details the relation analysis,
MaxSMT solving, and validation stages.

\subsection{Joint Trace Encoding}

MPTA-Repair uses the clock-bound variation representation introduced in
TARTAR~\cite{kolbl2019clock} as the basis for its joint multi-property
encoding and iterative repair procedure. Let $S$ denote the set of
repairable clock bounds. For each clock bound $s\in S$, the original
numerical bound $b_s$ in a transition guard or location invariant is
associated with a variation variable $\delta_s$. The repaired numerical
bound $b'_s$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  b'_s = b_s + \delta_s
\end{equation}

MPTA-Repair builds on the trace-encoding formulation of TARTAR and
extends it to the multi-property setting \cite{kolbl2019clock}.
Consider a TDT with $n$ discrete transitions:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \tau ={}&
  \ell_0 \xrightarrow{d_0,\theta_0} \ell_1 \cdots \\
  &{}\xrightarrow{d_{n-1},\theta_{n-1}} \ell_n
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $d_i$ is the delay before transition $\theta_i$, where
$\theta_i=(\ell_i,g_i,a_i,R_i,\ell_{i+1})$.
Let $\rho=(b'_s)_{s\in S}$ denote the modified-bound vector, and let
$\mathbf{z}_{\tau}=(d_0,\ldots,d_{n-1},\nu_0,\ldots,\nu_n)$ collect
the delay values and clock valuations along this execution, where
$\nu_i$ is the clock valuation at location $\ell_i$. Then the trace path
is encoded as a conjunction of linear arithmetic constraints:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \operatorname{Path}(\tau,\rho,\mathbf{z}_{\tau})
  ={}& \mathit{Init}(\nu_0)\\
  &{}\land
  \bigwedge_{i=0}^{n-1}\Bigl(
    d_i \ge 0
    \land \mathit{Inv}(\ell_i,\nu_i+d_i,\rho)\\
  &{}\land
    \mathit{Guard}(\theta_i,\nu_i+d_i,\rho)\\
  &{}\land
    \nu_{i+1}
    =
    \mathit{Reset}(\nu_i+d_i,R_i)
  \Bigr)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathit{Init}(\nu_0)$ constrains the initial clock valuation,
$\mathit{Inv}$ evaluates the invariant $I(\ell_i)$, and
$\mathit{Guard}$ evaluates the guard $g_i$ of transition $\theta_i$
under the given valuation and modified bounds. The reset equation
$\nu_{i+1}=\mathit{Reset}(\nu_i+d_i,R_i)$ updates the valuation after
transition $\theta_i$ by resetting clocks in $R_i$ and preserving the
others.
The predicate $\mathit{Bad}_{\varphi}(\ell_n,\nu_n)$ characterizes the
violation state of $\varphi$ at the final location. To block this
violating behavior while preserving executability of the discrete path
skeleton, the single-trace repair constraint uses two disjoint copies
$\mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{+}$ and $\mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{-}$ of the delay and
clock-valuation variables:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  &\Psi_{\tau,\varphi}^{\mathrm{repair}}(\rho)
  =
  \exists \mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{+}.
  \operatorname{Path}(\tau,\rho,\mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{+}) \\
  &\quad{}\land
  \neg \exists \mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{-}.
  \Bigl(
    \operatorname{Path}(\tau,\rho,\mathbf{z}_{\tau}^{-}) \\
  &\qquad{}\land
    \mathit{Bad}_{\varphi}(\ell_n,\nu_n^{-})
  \Bigr)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The first part requires that the original discrete path skeleton still
has at least one timed realization after repair. This preserves the
observed discrete behavior: the repair must not remove the transition
sequence simply to avoid the violation. The second part requires that no
assignment of delays and clock valuations can make that same path
violate $\varphi$.

We extend this single-trace formulation to a joint, multi-property
incremental setting. At refinement round $k$, counterexamples from all
currently violated properties are accumulated into a trace pool
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}_k=\{(\tau_j,\varphi_j)\}_{j=1}^{m_k}
\end{equation}
where $m_k=|\mathcal{T}_k|$ is the number of property-trace pairs in
the pool at round $k$. MPTA-Repair assembles a joint MaxSMT problem with
the constraint system defined as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal{F}_k(\rho)
  =
  &\mathit{Dom}(\rho)
  \land \mathit{Block}_k(\rho) \\
  &{}\land
  \bigwedge_{(\tau,\varphi)\in \mathit{Select}(\mathcal{T}_k)}
  \Psi_{\tau,\varphi}^{\mathrm{repair}}(\rho)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathit{Dom}(\rho)$ enforces static interval consistency and
domain limits, such as $b'_s\ge 0$; $\mathit{Block}_k(\rho)$ collects
the blocking clauses accumulated before round $k$ and is initially
true; and $\mathit{Select}(\mathcal{T}_k)$ represents a reduced subset
of traces obtained after relation-aware filtering. When an instantiated
candidate NTA fails verification, the constraint system is updated
incrementally. The framework expands the pool via
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}_{k+1}
  =
  \mathcal{T}_k
  \cup
  \mathit{NewTDTs}(N(\rho_k),\Phi)
\end{equation}
where $\rho_k$ is the modified-bound assignment produced in round $k$,
$N(\rho_k)$ is the candidate network obtained by applying $\rho_k$ to
$N$, and $\mathit{NewTDTs}(N(\rho_k),\Phi)$ is the set of newly exposed
property-trace pairs returned by full-property verification. The
framework then updates the search-space block to exclude the failed
assignment $\rho_k$ over the set of repairable bounds $S$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathit{Block}_{k+1}(\rho)
  =
  \mathit{Block}_k(\rho)
  \land
  \left(
    \bigvee_{s\in S} \delta_s \ne \delta_s^k
  \right)
\end{equation}
where $\delta_s^k$ is the valuation assigned to variation variable
$\delta_s$ in round $k$.

\subsection{Relation-Guided Search Optimization}

During refinement, accumulating violated properties, TDTs, and
repairable bounds increases the size and complexity of the MaxSMT
problem. To improve solving efficiency, MPTA-Repair uses
relation-guided optimization to filter and group constraints, and to
rank repairable bounds. These heuristics only
guide the search; correctness is still guaranteed through full-property
regression verification and an admissibility check.

MPTA-Repair analyzes relations at the property, counterexample, and
repair-bound levels to reduce and organize the active MaxSMT problem.
Timed safety properties are grouped according to the state predicates
and clock constraints appearing in their $(\texttt{A[]},p)$ queries;
identical queries are treated as duplicates, while tighter bounds are
given higher priority among properties with the same structure. Each
TDT is stored together with the property it violates, and
property-trace pairs that share transitions or repair variables are
grouped for joint solving. The framework then maps each active
property-trace constraint to the repairable clock-bound occurrences in
its encoding. A bound is given higher priority if it occurs in newly
added TDTs or is associated with more TDTs and violated properties. This
ranking guides active-variable selection and candidate generation,
whereas the number and magnitude of clock-bound changes are handled by
the MaxSMT optimization objective. To avoid repeated encoding across
iterations, MPTA-Repair applies quantifier elimination (QE) to each
property-trace constraint, eliminating its delay and trace-local clock
variables to obtain a quantifier-free constraint over the repair
variables. The resulting constraint is cached and reused in later
rounds, so QE is required only for newly added property-trace pairs. All
properties remain in $\Phi$ for full-property regression verification,
and a constraint is removed from the active encoding only when it is a
duplicate or logically implied by another active constraint.

The optimization objective is governed by a hierarchical set of soft
constraints. MPTA-Repair uses a lexicographic objective that first
favors small repairs and then incorporates risk- and coverage-based
priorities:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname*{lex\,min}_{\rho}
\left(
\begin{aligned}
  &\sum_{s\in S}\mathbf{1}\{\delta_s \ne 0\},\\
  &\sum_{s\in S}|\delta_s|,\\
  &\sum_{s\in S} \mathit{risk}_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\delta_s \ne 0\},\\
  &-\sum_{s\in S} \mathit{benefit}_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\delta_s \ne 0\}
\end{aligned}
\right)
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}$ is the indicator function. The first two
objectives minimize the number and total magnitude of altered clock
bounds. The last two terms penalize high-risk edits and prioritize
bounds covering more violated traces.
